Book Recap : Talking to Strangers
2020-04-27 01:02 label books  

This is the second Malcolm Gladwell book I’ve read so far. I read Outliers nearly a decade ago and thought that, while it certainly was an interesting read, it didn’t personally contain any ground-breaking insight as far as I was concerned. At the risk of sounding reductionist, I thought the conclusion that success, while often (mis)attributed to talent is always backed up by time invested by the person involved, was sort of a given.

I found Talking to Strangers much more enjoyable and insightful. The central thesis of this book is that generally, us .. human beings, are spectacularly and demonstrably bad at sizing up and gauging the true intention of strangers we interact with on a day to day basis. This major blind spot affects us all equally regardless of whether we’re corporate workers, cops, CIA agents, FBI agents, Prime Ministers, judges…the list goes on.

Gladwell’s central thesis builds around 3 relatively straight forward concepts

  1. For mostly evolutionary and society building reasons humans almost always assume that the person on the other side of the conversation is being truthful (originally proposed by a psychology study titled Truth Default Theory by Timothy Levine)
  2. We erroneously assume that true representations of people’s intentions are “readable” from their facial expressions and body language. Gladwell cites a wide variety of historical examples and scientific studies that help illustrate just how bad we are at reading other people. He also cites the popularity of the TV show Friends as a result of all characters being unambiguously and truthfully expressive.
  3. The idea of “coupling” which postulates that people’s behaviors(even in extreme cases like suicide) is closely linked with verify specific circumstances and conditions that are external to them rather than something intrinsic.

The last one sounded a bit too obvious to me, but I was surprised to read the examples he cited, like how suicide rate among women in the UK plummeted after UK switched to natural gas instead of coal-derived Carbon Monoxide laced cooking gas which apparently was an oft deployed suicide tool in the UK (victims include the likes of Slyvia Plath).

The book also took some informative detours to illustrate wider implications of our inability to accurately read strangers, including

  • Why “enhanced interrogation techniques” (fancy politically sterile phrase for torture) don’t work
  • How judges who routinely make “gut feel” calls about defendants based on their appearance before setting bail end up misreading intentions/guilt/remorse. In fact a study out of Harvard ended up proving that a pre-trained computer program can be demonstrably more accurate at setting bail terms than human judges.
  • Why the CIA took too long to detect Castro’s spies among its ranks for years
  • Deep dives into why authorities in places like Penn State University sat on allegations of sexual misconduct against its staff

All in all, I thought “Talking to Strangers” made for some insightful Sunday pop-science reading and I shall forever label people’s claims to be able to “read” other people’s intentions from behavioral ticks and quirks as “bullshit”
